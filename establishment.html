Establishment Survey

Monthly change in nonfarm payrolls, by industry

The establishment (CES) survey of payroll employment was not interrupted by the 2025 federal-government shutdown — every series here has continuous monthly data through the latest release. Toggles let you switch between monthly, 3-month, and 6-month moving averages.

Sectoral summary

The latest reading by sector against trailing averages and pre-pandemic norms, with sectoral wage growth alongside (production & nonsupervisory AHE).

The table is sortable on every column (click any header). Default sort is by the largest absolute change in the latest month so the biggest movers float to the top. Use the Level column to sort back into hierarchy: 0 = total nonfarm, 1 = total private / government, 2 = major group (goods / services), 3 = industry, 4 = sub-industry. The search box filters on any field — type “Goods” or “Services” to isolate one tree.

Sectoral contributions

Diffusion index

The CES diffusion index is the share of industries adding jobs in a given month (with industries unchanged counted at half-weight). Readings above 50 indicate broad-based hiring; readings below 50 indicate the gains are concentrated.

Share of private-sector gains

A Konczal-style framing: of the cumulative private-sector jobs added since December 2024, how much has gone to health care & social assistance vs the five blue-collar sectors (mining, construction, manufacturing, transport & warehousing, utilities) vs all other private industries combined? When gains are concentrated in health care, the headline NFP number is masking weakness elsewhere.

Blue-collar trend gap

Each panel fits a linear trend through cumulative job changes from December 2022 to December 2024, then projects it forward. The dashed trend line is what hiring would look like at the 2023–24 pace; the solid line is actual. Negative gap = jobs below trend.

Cyclical sensitivity tertiles

Sectors grouped (manually, by conventional economic sensitivity) into high, medium and low cyclical buckets. Rolling 3-month averages of monthly payroll change. The high-sensitivity tertile drops first in slowdowns and recovers fastest; the low-sensitivity tertile (mostly health, education and government) is much steadier.
